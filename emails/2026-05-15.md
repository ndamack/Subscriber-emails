Subject: The Button That Does Absolutely Nothing (And You've Pressed It A Million Times)

You've been lied to your whole life.

Not by a politician. Not by a conspiracy theorist lurking in a comments section.

By an elevator.

That "close door" button you've been frantically jabbing when you're late for a meeting?

Yeah. It doesn't work.

In most elevators across the US, that button is either completely disabled or programmed to have zero effect — because the Americans with Disabilities Act requires doors to stay open long enough for people with mobility challenges to safely board.

The button was never meant to close anything.

It exists for one reason only: to make *you* feel in control.

And here's the truly wild part — it works like a charm.

Psychologists call this a "placebo button," and elevators are far from alone in this little deception.

Many crosswalk buttons in major cities? Also disconnected. Installed in the 1970s, never rewired.

That office thermostat everyone wages cold war over? Often just decorative plastic on a wall.

Turns out, humans are remarkably easy to keep calm.

Hand us a button and we feel powerful, purposeful, and completely in charge — even when nothing is happening.

Next time you step into an elevator, watch a stranger stab that button with laser-focused determination.

Smile to yourself.

You know something they don't.

And honestly? That little secret might be the most useful thing you learn all week.
