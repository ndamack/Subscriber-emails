Subject: The Flying Monk Who Beat Halley By 700 Years

Picture this: a monk, a tower, homemade wooden wings, and absolutely zero hesitation.

It's 1010 AD, and Eilmer of Malmesbury has decided he is going to fly.

He straps wings to his hands and feet, climbs to the top of his English abbey tower, and jumps.

He actually glides for about 200 metres.

Then he crash-lands and breaks both his legs.

Most people would stop there.

Eilmer does not stop there.

He reportedly wants to try again — this time with a tail for stability — but his abbot says absolutely not.

Here's where the story gets genuinely wild.

As a child in 989 AD, Eilmer had seen a blazing comet tear across the sky.

Then, in 1066 AD, he sees it again.

He looks up at the same comet returning and reportedly says: "You've come again... you carrier of bad luck to many mothers."

He *recognises* it.

Same comet. Two appearances. Seventy-seven years apart.

Edmond Halley wouldn't formally identify that pattern until 1705.

Eilmer beat him to it by nearly 700 years.

A man who broke both his legs leaping off a tower was also one of history's most overlooked astronomers.

Research published this month confirmed it: Eilmer's observation was the real deal.

Sometimes the people most ahead of their time are the ones with the most spectacular crash landings.

Never let anyone tell you that falling means you weren't flying.
