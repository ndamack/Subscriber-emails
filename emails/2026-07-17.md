Subject: Someone checked if cancer research was real. The results are alarming.

What if the cures we've been waiting decades for were built on quicksand?

A team of researchers just did something nobody had dared to do at scale.

They built an AI and pointed it at 2.6 million cancer research papers.

Every paper published between 1999 and 2024.

It came back with a finding that made scientists go very quiet.

More than 250,000 of those papers show suspicious writing patterns.

Patterns that match "paper mills" — shady operations that sell fake, fabricated, or manipulated scientific manuscripts to researchers who need to publish.

Back in the early 2000s, roughly 1% of cancer papers looked dodgy.

By 2022? That number had climbed to more than 16%.

One in six.

Let that sink in for a moment.

The researchers aren't calling every flagged paper an outright fraud — the AI identifies patterns, not certainties.

But the trend is impossible to ignore.

Three scientific journals are already using this tool to screen papers before they ever get published.

Because somewhere between a scientist having a genuine breakthrough and you reading a headline about a "promising new treatment"... the system got flooded.

With noise. With noise wearing a lab coat.

The team is from Queensland University of Technology, and they used BERT — the same kind of language model that powers your phone's autocomplete.

They turned it into a scientific lie detector.

And what it found is that cancer research has a massive, growing, and surprisingly quiet problem.

Today I learned that science has been checking its own homework.

The homework was not great.
