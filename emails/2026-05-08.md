Subject: Neanderthals Died Alone. You Didn't. Here's Why.

Picture this: 40,000 years ago, two species are fighting for survival in Ice Age Europe.

One has bigger brains and stronger bodies.

The other has... a group chat.

Guess which one you're descended from.

Scientists just cracked the mystery of why Neanderthals vanished while Homo sapiens thrived.

It wasn't climate change. Neanderthals had survived dozens of brutal ice ages before.

It wasn't intelligence. Their tools were sophisticated, their brains large.

The secret weapon? Social connectivity.

Researchers from Université de Montréal modelled the population networks of both species across Ice Age Europe.

What they found was jaw-dropping.

Homo sapiens formed wide, flexible, interregional networks — almost like an ancient internet.

When a crisis hit, they could ping a tribe 200 miles away: "Hey, the mammoths moved. Come join us."

Neanderthals? Their networks were patchy. Fragile. Isolated pockets of people with weak links to the outside world.

One bad winter. One drought. One disease. And a whole population just... vanished.

With no one to call. No one to trade with. No safety net.

Meanwhile, Homo sapiens were out here building the world's first social network — and it saved the entire species.

Every time you text a friend, call in a favour, or show up for someone who needed it — you're doing exactly what kept your ancestors alive.

Connection isn't just nice to have. It's literally the reason you exist.

So, next time someone says "just stay home and don't bother anyone" — feel free to remind them that approach killed off an entire species.
